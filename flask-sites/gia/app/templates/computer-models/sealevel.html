Computer Models

Computer models are programs written to simulate real world systems based on our understanding of the physical laws that govern them. These laws are expressed as mathematical equations which, given the required inputs, can be solved to produce an approximation of some other state of the system in the form of output variable values.

Here you can learn more about the computational modelling that goes into our understanding of past, present and future sea level rise. This section also discusses the input requirements for each model. Click below to learn how these models work together to help us predict sea level.

Sea Level Model

Ice Distribution

Ice-sheets that existed thousands of years ago covered a far greater area and were much thicker than those found today. When calculating changes in sea level it is important to understand how the shape and thickness of past ice-sheets changed with time.

Earth Geometry

Changes in the geometry of the Earth's surface can cause some ocean basins to become deeper and others to become shallower in addition to changing the shape of the ocean itself. Understanding why and how this geometry changes through time is a key component of sea level modelling.

Earth Rheology

While it may seem solid to us, whether the Earth is in fact a solid object or a viscous liquid is all down to the time scale that it is viewed from. Understanding the rheology of the Earth helps us to predict how it will deform over tens of thousands of years.
